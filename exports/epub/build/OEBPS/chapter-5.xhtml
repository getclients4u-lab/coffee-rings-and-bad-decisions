Chapter 5 — The Almost-Kiss and the Espresso Explosion

By day seven of the partnership, Maya had developed a theory: Leo was not a bad person. He was a person who had made a series of bad decisions that had led him to a job he didn't want, in a company he didn't believe in, doing things that made him wake up at 3 a.m. and wonder how he'd ended up here.

This theory was based entirely on the fact that he'd shown up at 6:30 a.m. every day for a week, that he'd sorted three hundred pounds of green coffee beans into burlap sacks with the focused attention of someone defusing a bomb, and that he'd stopped wearing the navy suit.

The navy suit had been replaced, on day four, by a gray sweater that Maya vaguely recognized from a Gap she'd once been inside. The sweater had a coffee stain on the sleeve that he'd tried to hide but that Maya had pointed out immediately because Maya was the kind of person who noticed coffee stains the way a hawk noticed a field mouse.

The stain was from yesterday. Maya had thrown a handful of grounds at him during a playful argument about roast profiles. She regretted it. She also didn't.

"You're overthinking the grind," Leo said, on day eight.

They were standing at the espresso machine. Maya was making a latte. Leo was watching her with the particular intensity of someone who'd spent three years analyzing supply chains and had now redirected that energy toward analyzing tamping pressure.

"I am not overthinking the grind," Maya said. "You are overthinking the grind. You've been here for eight days and you've already commented on my grind eleven times."

"Your grind is inconsistent. On Monday it was too fine. On Tuesday it was too coarse. Today it's somewhere in between but trending toward fine, which will over-extract and make the latte bitter."

Maya finished the latte. She set it on the counter. She looked at Leo. She looked at the steam wand, which was currently hissing like a cat that had been surprised. She looked at the window, where the sun was doing that thing it did in Portland in June where it decided to show up for three hours and then leave like a guest who'd realized the party wasn't for them.

"Let's do a cupping," she said.

Leo blinked. "A cupping."

"A cupping. You know. Where we taste different roasts and compare notes and see who has the better palate."

Leo smiled. It was a dangerous smile. The kind of smile that suggested he knew something she didn't, or that he'd been waiting for exactly this challenge. "You're on."

They moved to the back room, to the small table where Maya usually did paperwork and Sam usually ate lunch. Maya laid out five small glass cups on a tray, each with a different roast, and placed a silver spoon beside each one. She poured hot water over the grounds. The smell filled the room — rich and dark and alive, the kind of smell that made you want to close your eyes and just breathe.

"Start with the light roast," Maya said. "Work your way down."

Leo took the first cup. He slurped. He made a face. "Blueberry. Chocolate. A hint of — is that cedar?"

Maya's eyebrows went up. Cedar was a tasting note that appeared on exactly one bag in her inventory, and she'd never told anyone about it because the one person who'd asked had said "this tastes like a woodshop" and Maya had said "it's called terroir, Brenda, look it up."

"That's the Ethiopia," she said.

Leo nodded like he'd expected it. He moved to the second cup. Third. Fourth. Each time he got closer to the right notes. Each time Maya found herself leaning in, watching his face, reading his reactions.

By the fifth cup, the darkest roast, the one she called "Midnight," Leo was quiet. He took a sip. He held it. He swallowed slowly, like he was trying to catch something that was fleeing.

"What do you get?" Maya asked.

Leo opened his eyes. "You're not going to like it."

"Try me."

"Smoke. Bittersweet chocolate. And something underneath that tastes like the memory of fruit."

Maya stared at him. "The memory of fruit."

"Fruit that was there once and now is just a ghost."

"That's — that's exactly what I get."

Leo smiled. It was the smile again. The one that made Maya's stomach do the thing it had done yesterday when their fingers had brushed at the counter. "We make a good team," he said.

"Don't get cocky."

"I'm not being cocky. I'm being accurate. Your palate is exceptional. Most people taste 'coffee.' You taste the underneath."

There was a silence. The kind of silence that happened in rom-coms right before the almost-kiss. The kind of silence that involved leaning, and hesitation, and the sound of a refrigerator humming in the background, and a feeling in Maya's chest that was like when you missed a step on the stairs — not falling, just the moment before.

Leo leaned in. Maya leaned in.

The refrigerator door swung open.

A carton of oat milk fell out. It hit the floor with a sound that was less of a crash and more of a betrayed sigh.

They both jumped. Leo stumbled. Maya laughed — she couldn't help it. It came out as a snort, which was humiliating, but Leo laughed too, and suddenly they were both standing in the back room of a coffee shop on Alberta Street, covered in oat milk, laughing like they'd just won something that they hadn't known was a contest.

"That," Maya said, wiping her eyes, "is the least romantic thing that has ever happened to me."

"It's memorable," Leo said.

"It's a refrigerator."

"Refrigerators are underrated," Leo said. "That fridge just saved us from a moment that would have been awkward in about three different ways."

"How so?"

"Well," Leo said, and his voice dropped just slightly, "if we'd kissed, I would have thought it meant something. And you would have panicked. And we would have had to navigate that the next day while sorting beans. And we don't have time for that. We have a business to run."

Maya's smile faltered. He was right. She would have panicked. She would have spent the next week avoiding eye contact, and they'd lose momentum on the bean sale, and everything would fall apart.

"I appreciate the refrigerator's tactical intervention," she said.

"Appliances are tactical," Leo said. "They see things we don't."

Sam walked in at that moment. She took in the scene: Maya and Leo, side by side, covered in oat milk, laughing. The open refrigerator. The spilled carton on the floor.

"...did I miss something?" Sam asked.

"The refrigerator saved us from ourselves," Maya said.

"Of course it did. Fridge is a legend. Can I have a scone?"

"Help yourself," Maya said. "But you're cleaning the milk."

"Already on it."

Sam knelt to clean the milk. Maya looked at Leo. He looked at her. There was no more leaning. There was just work. But under the work, under the spreadsheet and the supply chain and the five hundred pounds of Ethiopian beans and the three weeks they had to sell them, there was something else. Something that had started with an argument about pour-over and had become, somehow, a partnership that felt like walking on a balance beam — terrifying and exhilarating and requiring every ounce of attention.

The bell jingled. The day continued. Maya made coffee. Leo watched. Sam ate a scone.

And somewhere in the back of Maya's mind, the refrigerator was watching too.

*End of Chapter 5*
