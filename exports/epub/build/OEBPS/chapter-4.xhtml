Chapter 4 — Mrs. Hsu's Coaster

Maya had never been fired. She had never been hired, technically — she'd bought the shop, which meant she'd hired herself, which meant she could fire herself but she never would because she needed the health insurance. The closest she'd come to professional humiliation was the time a customer had asked for a cortado and she'd made a latte and then claimed it was "an artistic interpretation and also it was still four dollars."

She had not been enjoying Leo's company the previous afternoon. She had, in fact, been actively resenting it. The fact that he'd shown up this morning at 6:30 a.m., ten minutes before opening, wearing a white shirt that was slightly too big and carrying a clipboard he'd apparently purchased at a drugstore on the way, did not improve her mood.

"I made a list," he said.

Maya stared at him over the espresso machine. "A list."

"Of things we can do with five hundred pounds of Ethiopian Yirgacheffe in thirty days. I've broken it into three phases. Phase one: immediate sales. Phase two: community events. Phase three: viral moments."

Maya's jaw clicked. "Viral moments."

"It's a strategy, not a lifestyle."

"It's a lifestyle for people who say 'strategy' in meetings."

Leo ignored this. He unfolded the clipboard and revealed a spreadsheet that was, Maya had to admit, extremely well-organized. Columns. Dates. Numbers. Colors she didn't understand but that looked important.

"Where did you get this?" she asked.

"Bean & Leaf. I had thirty minutes after the firing email before security escorted me out. I printed everything I could about our supply chain relationships. African exporters, West Coast roasters, the Seattle distribution hub that Bean & Leaf uses and that I can't use anymore because I'm excommunicated."

"Excommunicated."

"Corporate fired. Same thing. Look — I know five roasters who need small-batch Ethiopian and can't get it because Bean & Leaf locked down the best contracts. I know three events happening in the next two weeks where coffee professionals will pay a premium for single-origin. I know a guy at the Saturday market who sells coffee out of a trailer and whose customers would buy beans from us just because we're not Bean & Leaf."

Maya read the spreadsheet. It was good. It was actually good. The kind of good that made her hands twitch because she hadn't had another adult to share operational thinking with since Sam, and Sam thought spreadsheets were "spread sheets" and that was the extent of her knowledge.

"How much did you sleep last night?" Maya asked.

"None."

"That's not sustainable."

"This is a startup. Startups don't sleep."

"This is a coffee shop. We sleep. We have to. The espresso machine has a sleep mode."

Leo looked at the espresso machine with genuine curiosity, as though it were a living creature. "Does it dream about milk?"

"No," Maya said. "It dreams about being cleaned more often."

The morning rush hit before they could continue the conversation. Maya worked the machine. Leo stood beside her, watching, and occasionally said things like "you're tamping too hard" and "your grind size is inconsistent" and "you're wasting milk," and each comment was accurate and also made Maya want to throw a tamper at his head.

By 11:00 a.m., the rush had thinned to a murmur. Sam arrived, carrying a tote bag and the particular energy of someone who'd had a very good idea and was waiting for the right moment to share it.

"Did you see the Bean & Leaf flyers?" Sam asked, dropping the tote bag on a table.

Maya's stomach dropped. "Flyers."

Sam produced a crumpled piece of paper from the tote bag. It was glossy. It featured a photo of a white mug on a wooden table with a plant beside it and a person with good posture drinking from it. The headline read: "Ethical Sourcing, Elevated."

"They're hiring," Sam said. "Barista positions. Twelve dollars an hour."

"Twelve dollars," Maya said. "That's what we pay."

"Bean & Leaf pays twelve dollars with health insurance and a loyalty program."

Maya looked at the flyer. She looked at Leo, who'd gone quiet. He was staring at the floor. He'd known this was coming. He'd known Bean & Leaf was expanding, but this — this was personal. This was them walking into her neighborhood and stealing her staff with a glossy flyer and a promise of stability.

"We need to match it," Sam said.

"We don't have insurance," Maya said. "We don't have a loyalty program. We have a paper card that we hole-punch."

"We can make a loyalty program," Sam said. "We can make it better. We can make it digital. I have a friend who's a web developer. We can do QR codes."

"We can't afford a developer," Maya said.

"I'll do it. I can build a simple app. I've been learning React."

"Sam," Maya said gently, "you once tried to fix the espresso machine and broke the steam wand."

"That was one time. And I fixed it."

"You superglued the steam wand to the boiler."

"It held."

Maya looked at Leo. He was still quiet. She expected him to say something smooth, something corporate about "synergies" or "partnerships" or "why don't we just work together and solve this problem." She'd been bracing for it. She was ready to reject it.

Instead, Leo said, "I know the roaster they're using. I can get a better one. We can offer exclusive beans that Bean & Leaf can't get because they're locked into contracts. We can be the shop that actually has the good stuff."

"You're the person who got fired," Maya said.

"I'm the person who knows where the good stuff is."

Maya looked around the shop. She looked at the chalkboard menu with its smudged letters. She looked at the espresso machine that Leo had just taught her to calibrate better. She looked at Sam, who was bouncing on her heels with a t-shirt idea that Maya could already tell was terrible but also kind of brilliant. She looked at the door where Mrs. Hsu had stood, telling her that Bean & Leaf was buying the pharmacy, telling her to write a business plan, telling her that she was not alone in this fight even though she certainly felt like it.

She thought about the spreadsheet in Leo's hand. She thought about the fourteen thousand pounds of beans in the warehouse. She thought about the bell above the door that had stopped jingling for exactly thirty seconds and then started again, as if to remind her that the shop was still alive, still breathing, still waiting.

"Okay," she said.

Leo's head came up. "Okay?"

"Okay. Phase one of the spreadsheet. We start today. We sell ten pounds by the end of the week. Twenty pounds by the end of next week. We hit forty by the end of the month. We make the deadline."

"Forty pounds," Leo said, looking at his math. "That's — that's aggressive."

"I know. That's why I picked it."

Sam whooped. It was a sound that had once been described by a customer as "joyful but alarming." "I'll make the loyalty cards. Physical ones. Because physical is better. And I'll start posting on Instagram. Real posts. Not the curated bullshit. Real stuff. The burnt pastries. The weird customers. The fact that we're a coffee shop fighting a corporate war and we're winning because we have better beans."

"Sam," Maya said, "you can't say we're winning before we've won anything."

"We're already winning. Because we're trying," Sam said.

Leo closed the spreadsheet. He looked at Maya with an expression that was not the corporate smile and not the charming grin but something in between, something more honest, something that said "I don't believe in this either but I'm going to try." "I'll start calling the roasters."

"I'll start making better coffee," Maya said.

"And I'll start not breaking things," Sam said.

Maya laughed. It was a short, sharp sound that surprised her because it meant she was still capable of laughter even when her loan was due and her competition had just opened a store three blocks away and she'd just hired the man who'd been sent to destroy her.

The bell jingled. A customer walked in. Maya served them. Leo watched, and then he asked a question about her tamping technique, and Maya told him it was "a proprietary method," and Leo said he understood proprietary methods because he'd spent three years at Bean & Leaf pretending to care about corporate synergy, and Maya said she'd spent three years pretending to care about latte art, and they both looked at Sam, who said "I knew you two would get along. I'm getting nachos."

The morning continued. The espresso machine hissed. The fridge hummed its B-flat. The chalkboard menu stayed smudged. Maya served coffee, Leo watched, Sam ate scones, and for exactly four hours, the shop felt like something that might survive.

Not because of the spreadsheet. Not because of the plan. But because for the first time since she'd bought the place, Maya wasn't alone.

She had a partner. A partner she'd rather not have. A partner who was technically her enemy. A partner who was currently drinking her coffee and telling her tamping jokes and standing at her counter like he belonged there.

He didn't belong there. She knew that. He knew that.

But the shop didn't seem to mind.

*End of Chapter 4*
