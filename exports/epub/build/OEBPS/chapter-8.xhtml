Chapter 8 — TikTok, Torrents, and Leo's Bad Hand

The drink was at a bar on Alberta called The Record Room, which had a jukebox full of vinyl and a bartender named Jules who remembered everyone's order and who'd once told Maya that her espresso was "better than anything I can make in a cup" and Maya had framed the compliment mentally and lived off it for six months.

They sat at the bar. Leo drank a negroni. Maya drank a negroni because she was tired of being predictable. The first sip was terrible. The second sip was interesting. By the third, she was describing her business model to Jules in detail.

"We have fourteen thousand pounds of beans," Maya said.

"Pounds," Jules said, wiping a glass.

"Pounds. Fourteen thousand dollars of beans. Twenty-one days to sell them. We've sold forty-three pounds in one day. Do the math."

Jules did the math. He shook his head. "You're behind."

"I know. We're behind. But we got a boost. The festival. Leo's spreadsheet. Leo being Leo, apparently."

Leo winced. "Being Leo isn't a market advantage."

"It is right now. Your spreadsheet saved us."

"I'm not a spreadsheet," Leo said. "I'm a person."

"Your spreadsheet is very personable."

Leo laughed. The sound was different in the bar — softer, less strategic, more like the laugh of someone who'd forgotten, for a moment, that they'd been fired three days ago.

Maya liked that laugh. She liked it more than she should.

"Tell me about Bean & Leaf," she said.

Leo's smile faltered. "What about it."

"You know their supply chain. You know their secrets. Was it always so — corporate?"

Leo took a sip of the negroni. He looked at the jukebox, which was playing something that sounded like a song from 1974 that had been waiting forty years to be heard again. "Bean & Leaf started with good intentions. The founder, Tom, he actually cared about coffee. He was a roaster. He'd been in the industry for twenty years. He believed in direct trade before direct trade was a buzzword."

"What happened?"

"VC money."

Maya nodded. She knew the story. It was the same story that happened to every good idea that got close enough to a venture capitalist to smell like money. "They scaled. The vision got diluted. The founder got pushed out. The people who replaced him cared about growth, not coffee."

"Bingo," Leo said. "Tom got fired two years ago. He's running a small roastery in Bend now. He sends me emails sometimes. He sends me coffee. He's the one who told me about the Yirgacheffe lot — the one Bean & Leaf locked down. That's how I knew you had the good stuff."

Maya thought about this. She thought about Tom in Bend, sending coffee to the man who'd been fired for defending a small shop. She thought about how the industry was full of people like Tom, people who cared but who'd been pushed out by people who didn't.

Leo was not like Tom. Leo had compromised. Leo had worked for Bean & Leaf for three years, had taken the severance, had tried to survive. But he'd also quit. He'd also stood in Maya's shop and asked for a job and had meant it.

"We're not selling," Maya said.

Leo looked at her. "Selling what."

"The shop. Bean & Leaf offered. Indirectly. Through Rachel. She came to the festival and dropped hints. I'm not selling. Not now. Not ever."

Leo was quiet for a moment. Then he said, "Good."

"Good?"

"Good. Because if you sold, I'd have to find another coffee shop to work at, and I like yours. The espresso machine has personality."

Maya laughed. She laughed because it was a strange, genuine thing to say. She laughed because she was sitting in a bar with a man who'd been her enemy three weeks ago, drinking negronis, talking about coffee, and feeling something that she couldn't name but that was warm and real and not at all like the loneliness she'd been living with for three years.

"I have a question," she said.

"Shoot."

"Are you going to stay?"

Leo's face changed. Not much. Just a tightening around the eyes, a small recalculation. "Stay where."

"In the shop. After the beans are sold. After the three weeks. Are you going to stay."

Leo was quiet. The jukebox changed songs. Jules was wiping glasses, listening with the attentiveness of someone who'd heard a thousand conversations and had learned to read people like menus.

"Three weeks is the deal," Leo said.

"The deal was three weeks. I'm asking about after."

Leo took a breath. "I don't know. The Seattle job —"

"The Seattle job is in Seattle."

"It pays well."

"So does selling coffee. Eventually."

"It's not about the money."

"It's about what?"

Leo didn't answer immediately. He looked at the negroni in his glass. He looked at Maya. There was something in his eyes that Maya recognized — the particular conflict of someone who wanted two things that were in opposite directions, who was trying to choose between safety and something harder and more real.

"I've been playing it safe," he said. "For three years. I took the job at Bean & Leaf because it was the safe move. I stayed because it was the safe move. I let them fire me because I stopped caring, which was also a kind of safe, because I'd stopped fighting earlier."

"You're not playing it safe anymore," Maya said.

"No," Leo said. "I'm not."

He met her eyes. The silence stretched. Maya felt the pull again — the same pull she'd felt at the counter when their fingers had brushed, the same pull she'd felt in the back room before the refrigerator had saved them. It was the pull of proximity, of recognition, of two people who'd been alone for a long time and had accidentally found each other in the middle of a bean-selling emergency.

Jules slid another drink down the bar. Leo smiled. Maya didn't. She was too busy trying not to lean across the bar and kiss him, which was a terrible idea for at least seven reasons and a good idea for at least one reason that was currently winning.

"Another round?" Jules asked.

"Please," Maya said.

Leo smiled. He didn't lean across the bar. He didn't have to. The look he gave her said enough.
