# Chapter 17 — The Fight, Part Two

Leo came back the next morning.

He walked in at 6:32 a.m., same as always, with a thermos and a face that said I didn't sleep, and he made coffee like nothing had happened. But it had happened. Maya could feel it in the air, in the way he avoided her eyes, in the way he was meticulous and careful and not-quite-present.

She held it together until 11:00 a.m., when a customer asked if Leo was "the new owner."

"New owner," Maya repeated, the words coming out sharp. "No. He is not the new owner. He is a partner. And partners don't own things. Partners just — they help. Temporarily. Until they don't."

She regretted it before she finished speaking. Leo's face was closed. He set down the portafilter. He took off the apron.

"I'll go," he said.

"Don't go."

"I'm in the way."

"You're not in the way."

"I took the Seattle job."

Maya went cold. "What."

"I took it. This morning. Before I came here."

Maya stared at him. "You — you didn't tell me."

"I wasn't going to tell you," Leo said. "I thought it was better for you to hear it this way. I thought — I thought it was better for you if I just left quietly. If the beans sold. If the shop survived. And if it didn't, you'd have the buyout money."

"I don't want the buyout money," Maya said. Her voice rose. "I don't want you making decisions for me. You don't get to decide what's better for me. You don't get to decide that I need Seattle or a salary or whatever it is you think I need."

"I'm trying."

"Trying is not the same as staying."

Leo's jaw tightened. "I can't stay, Maya. Not if this doesn't work out. What if the shop fails? What if Bean & Leaf wins? I need a backup. I need —"

"I need you to trust me," Maya said. "I don't need you to be my backup. I need you to be my partner."

Leo shook his head. "That's not fair. You don't get to define my terms."

"That's exactly what you get to do when you're in a partnership."

They were standing three feet apart. The espresso machine hissed. A customer at table four looked up from their laptop. Sam was in the back room, and Maya could hear music — a song that was too loud and too happy for the moment.

"You know what the problem is," Maya said, her voice quieter now, "the problem is that you think love is something you can plan for. That you can put in a spreadsheet and color-code and predict. You can't. Love is — love is like a cup of coffee you make at 9 p.m. because you had a fight and you don't want to go to bed angry, and it's terrible, and you drink it anyway, and somehow it's better because someone else is drinking it with you."

She paused. She'd meant to say less than that. She'd meant to say "stay," but the words had run away from her, carrying the truth with them.

Leo was quiet. He looked at the floor. He looked at the espresso machine. He looked at her.

"I'm sorry," he said. "I have to go."

He walked out.

Maya stood in the doorway and watched him go. She didn't call after him. She didn't run after him. She let the bell jingle and fade and let the street swallow him up.

She was alone.

But she wasn't alone. Sam was in the back room. Mrs. Hsu owned the building. Jake was writing about her. The loyalty cards were scanning. The chalkboard was full of names.

She was alone, and she was not alone.

She closed the door.
