# Chapter 19 — The Crisis

Week eight.

Bean & Leaf opened the pharmacy location on Monday. The sign went up at 7:00 a.m. Maya saw it from the window. She stared at it for a long time. It was white and new and had a logo that was not her grandfather's parrot and not Mrs. Hsu's building and not the speckled floor of the speakeasy that had once been a bakery.

She served coffee that day. She made it better than she had in weeks. She talked to customers. She smiled. She forgot to eat lunch.

By Thursday, revenue was down eighteen percent.

"People are curious," Sam said on Friday. "They're going to try the new place. They'll come back."

"They won't," Maya said.

"Maya."

"They won't. Bean & Leaf has better everything. Better branding. Better Wi-Fi. Better everything except coffee, and most people can't tell the difference."

Sam didn't argue. Sam couldn't argue, because she'd been to the Bean & Leaf pharmacy location. She'd ordered a cortado. She'd said it was fine. She'd said it was better than fine, actually, which was why she was now quiet.

Maya didn't blame her. Maya didn't blame anyone. She blamed herself. She'd bought a shop she couldn't afford. She'd ordered five hundred pounds of beans she couldn't sell. She'd fallen for a man who'd taken a job in Seattle. She'd made every decision that had led her here.

The Thursday night after Bean & Leaf opened, Maya stayed late. She stood at the counter and looked at the empty shop and thought about selling. She thought about Rachel's offer. She thought about the bank account, which was healthier than it had been three months ago but was about to take a nosedive.

The bell jingled.

She looked up.

Mrs. Hsu was standing there. She was wearing the cardigan. She was holding a paper bag.

"I brought you something," Mrs. Hsu said.

She placed the bag on the counter. Inside was heat. A dish of something warm and fragrant and alive. Pineapple fried rice, Maya realized, with the particular precision of someone who'd been making this dish for forty years.

"Eat," Mrs. Hsu said. "You look like you haven't eaten in a week."

"I haven't," Maya said.

"Eat."

Maya sat at the table. She ate the rice. It was hot and sweet and sharp and tasted like Mrs. Hsu's kitchen, which was a kitchen that had been cooking for a very long time and had learned things that Maya was still learning.

"Bean & Leaf," Mrs. Hsu said, sitting across from her. "They're not your competition."

"They have a location three blocks away."

"They have a location. They don't have this." Mrs. Hsu tapped the table. The tap was faint, but it was there. "This table. Your chalkboard. The way you remember that I take my coffee black and don't let it steep too long. Bean & Leaf doesn't know I exist."

Maya smiled despite herself.

"They don't know you exist because you don't buy beans from them," Mrs. Hsu said. "But they know I exist. I told them."

Maya's spoon stopped. "You what."

"I told them I own the building. I told them the rent is eight hundred dollars and it's not going up. I told them if they try anything, I'll remind every customer who walks through my building what it feels like to support people who actually know their beans."

Maya stared at her. "Mrs. Hsu."

"Don't Mrs. Hsu me. You've been carrying this alone. You think you're alone. You're not. I'm here. Sam is here. Even Jake, apparently, is writing a Substack about you. This shop has a community. Bean & Leaf has an app. Apps don't write Substack essays."

Maya laughed. It was a wet, messy laugh. She covered her mouth with her hand. "I'm scared," she said.

"I know," Mrs. Hsu said. "Scared is good. Scared means you're trying something that matters."

Mrs. Hsu finished her coffee. She stood. She took the empty dish.

"Don't let the cold get to you," she said.

When she was gone, Maya sat in the empty shop for a long time. The refrigerator hummed. The streetlights outside turned on. The shop was quiet, but it wasn't empty. It was full of things that mattered. The chalkboard. The espresso machine. The table where Leo had sat. The gate that led to the back room where the four hundred pounds of coffee beans were waiting.

She stood. She walked to the back room. She opened the first sack. She breathed in the smell.

She thought about Seattle. About Leo's resignation letter. About the phone call that had ended with silence.

She thought about the espresso machine, which was quiet but alive, the way she felt when she was making coffee for people who mattered.

She opened the laptop. She started writing.

Not a business plan. Not a spreadsheet. A menu. A new menu. With the Yirgacheffe front and center. With notes about where the beans came from, who grew them, what the soil was like, what the weather had been that year. A menu that told a story.

She wrote until her eyes burned. She wrote until the streetlights outside turned off. She wrote until the refrigerator hummed the same note it always hummed, and the espresso machine slept, and the chalkboard waited for tomorrow.

She closed the laptop. She looked at the empty chair where Leo used to sit.

"One day at a time," she said.

"One cup at a time," said the silence.

She locked the door.

*End of Chapter 19*

The zoning pause didn't end the threat, but it changed the shape of it. Instead of a pharmacy arriving in eight weeks, Bean & Leaf now had to wait for a public comment period that could last up to sixty days. That gave Maya and Leo time to strengthen the business. Maya refined the subscription model with three tiers — Explorer, Devotee, and Believer — and launched it with a launch party that local media covered. The party had a band and a silent auction and a photo booth where people could take pictures with the chalkboard dragon. Leo wore a t-shirt that said "I <3 Mystery Beans" and stood behind a table answering questions about the subscription logistics. When a Bean & Leaf representative showed up uninvited, Leo greeted him by name, asked him what he thought of the event, and handed him a complimentary Mystery Bean bag. The representative left without purchasing a subscription. The episode became a TikTok moment, edited by Lila with a dramatic score.

The TikTok edit by Lila got 1.2 million views in forty-eight hours. Maya watched the numbers climb while standing at the counter. She handed out free Mystery Bean t-shirts to the first fifty customers. By noon, the shop was so busy that Sam had to start a waiting list. The online subscription page went live with a countdown timer. In the last hour, Maya and Leo sat on the floor of the storage room and watched the numbers tick up on a laptop. At one minute, they were at 187. At zero, they were at 228. Leo said, "We did it." Maya said, "We're just starting." They kissed. It was a quick, celebratory kiss, the kind that says: I told you so.

The day after the TikTok edit went viral, Maya woke up to a notification that her phone was dying. She'd left it charging, but the notifications had kept coming. By the time she got to the shop at 6:47, Leo was already there, sitting on the floor with the laptop and the subscription page open, watching numbers. He looked up. He said, "We crossed thirty thousand views an hour ago." Maya said, "How many subscriptions?" Leo said, "Two hundred and thirty-seven." Maya stood very still. Two hundred thirty-seven subscriptions at ninety dollars each was almost $21,000 — enough to cover the loan payment and leave a cushion. She sat down next to Leo on the floor. They didn't say anything. They just looked at the screen. The numbers kept changing.

On the morning of the subscription launch, Maya opened the shop at 6:45 a.m. and stood at the counter and looked at the clock. She had fifteen minutes before the first customer arrived. She had five minutes to herself. She spent them making coffee for Leo, who was still setting up the camera in the corner. The TikTok stream started at 11:17 a.m. Leo held the phone. Maya spoke to it. She said, "Today, we save the shop. And we build something that lasts." She didn't plan the words. They came out like a promise. The stream got 50,000 viewers in the first hour. By 2 p.m., it was at 180,000. By 5 p.m., it was at 450,000. The subscriptions kept coming. Maya and Leo counted them on a whiteboard. At 8:47 p.m., when the stream ended and the page closed, they sat on the floor and stared at the board. The number was 237. They had done it. They had actually done it. Leo said, "We beat them." Maya said, "Not yet. We just outlasted them for one day." She was right. But it was enough. It was enough for that day.

The following Saturday, Leo organized the first official Mystery Bean subscription pop-up. He turned the sidewalk outside the shop into a mini-market: folding tables, branded banners, a photo booth, and a signing area for the 237 new subscriptions. Maya had never seen anything like it. People were lined up at 11 a.m. for a bag of beans and a handshake from Leo. Within three hours, they had signed up another 102 subscriptions. At 3 p.m., a man from a local roastery showed up with a complimentary large-bag donation, saying he'd seen the TikTok and wanted to be part of it. By 6 p.m., the total stood at 339 new subscriptions for the day. Total revenue from subscriptions: $30,510. Maya looked at the spreadsheet and said, quietly, to herself, "We're going to make it." Leo, overhearing, said, "Hell yes we are.

By 10 a.m., the stream had 350,000 viewers. By noon, 600,000. By 3 p.m., the subscription page was getting 100 hits per minute. Maya and Leo sat on the floor of the storage room with laptops and whiteboards and a bag of chips. They divided the room into zones: Maya handled customer emails, Leo handled subscription logistics. Sam ran the front. Tom ran the roasting demonstration in the alley. Lila handled the camera. The energy was frantic and joyful. At 4:23 p.m., a delivery truck arrived with 500 pounds of new green coffee, donated by Rosa in Guatemala. Maya signed for it. She hugged the delivery driver. "Today is the best day of my life." The driver said, "Don't jinx it." Maya said, "I never jinx it. I make it." By closing, they had 339 subscriptions for the day, up from 237 at launch. Total new subscriptions: 576. Revenue: $51,840. Maya sent the bank a note: "We're not just paying the loan. We're buying the building." Jennifer replied: "Let's talk."

That night, Maya dreamed of a river made of coffee. She was floating on it, and Leo was beside her, and they were both laughing, and the river carried them past the shop and past the Bean & Leaf pharmacy and past the music venue and out to the ocean. She woke up at 6:04 a.m. She wrote the dream down. She texted Leo: "I had the coffee river dream." Leo replied: "I had the same one. We're psychically connected through caffeine." Maya said: "Obviously."
