# Chapter 16 — The Seattle Phone Call

The phone rang at 8:47 p.m.

Maya was in the shop alone. Leo had gone home to sleep. Sam was at a concert. Mrs. Hsu was wherever Mrs. Hsu went at night — probably her apartment upstairs from the bakery, probably watching old movies on a TV that she'd had since before Maya was born. The shop was quiet. The espresso machine had been turned off. The Mystery Bean tickets were in the wooden box on the counter. The subscription page was live and real and, as of 30 minutes ago, had seventy-two new subscribers.

Maya picked up the phone on the second ring. "Portland Coffee Rescue," she said. It was a joke. It had stopped being a joke a week ago.

"Maya," a voice said. A woman's voice. Older. Tense. "This is Elena. Leo's sister."

Maya's stomach dropped. "Is Leo okay."

"He's fine. He's at my apartment in Seattle. He's been here for three days."

Maya closed her eyes. "He didn't tell me he went to Seattle."

"He didn't tell anyone. He quit his job on Monday — formally, officially, with the two-week notice he's required to give — and then he drove here. He's been here for three days. He hasn't called you."

Maya felt the room tilt. "Why."

"He's been thinking. About a lot of things. About the shop. About us. About whether he did the right thing."

"Did he?"

"He doesn't know. That's why I'm calling. I need you to tell him he can come home. I need you to tell him he's not making a mistake."

Maya looked around the shop. She looked at the chalkboard dragon Sam had drawn. She looked at the Mystery Bean tickets. She looked at the window, where her reflection stared back, tired and determined and, for the first time in months, not entirely alone.

"Tell him to call me," she said.

"I did. He said he would if you asked him to."

"Then I'm asking."

"Good," Elena said. "I'll tell him."

The line went dead. Maya stood there for a long time with the phone in her hand. She thought about Leo leaving. She thought about him driving to Seattle, to his sister, to the place he'd grown up, the place he'd left because he'd wanted to be in a city that had coffee shops like this one. She thought about the kiss in the kitchen, the almost-kisses, the fight on Day Nineteen.

She thought: I have to fix this.

She thought: Maybe it's not broken.

She sat at the kitchen table upstairs. She opened her laptop. She started typing. She didn't know what she was writing — not a plan, not an email, just words, the kind of words that came out when you were thinking about someone you cared about and the future you might have together. She wrote about the shop. She wrote about the fight. She wrote about the kiss. She wrote about the 500-pound emergency and the lottery and the TikTok and the way the shop had become, against all odds, something more than she'd ever imagined.

She wrote: "I need you here. Not because we have a plan. Because we have something. Something that's worth fighting for. Something that's worth staying for."

She read it. It was true. It was honest. It was the kind of thing she'd never written before, not to anyone, not even to Sam.

She didn't send it. She saved it. She closed the laptop. She went to the window. She looked down at Alberta Street. The Thai restaurant was lit. The bookstore was dark. The Bean & Leaf pharmacy location was a hole in the ground, surrounded by construction fencing, covered in a banner that said COMING SPRING 2027. The banner made her angry. It also made her more determined.

At 9:12, her phone buzzed. Leo.

She picked up. "Hello."

"Maya. Elena told you I was here."

"I did. You didn't have to drive to Seattle to avoid me."

"I didn't drive to Seattle to avoid you. I drove to Seattle to think."

"About what."

"About whether I made a mistake by quitting. About whether I made a mistake by believing in you. About whether this — this shop, these beans, you — is real enough to build a life on."

Maya closed her eyes. "Yes. It's real."

"How do you know."

"Because I'm here. I'm still here. I didn't take Bean & Leaf's offer. I didn't chicken out. I stayed. And if you think I did that because I'm stubborn and not because I believe in something, then you don't know me very well."

Leo was quiet. "I'm coming back."

"Good."

"I have a condition."

"Conditions are for rent agreements."

"This isn't a rent agreement. It's a partnership."

Maya smiled. "A partnership."

"We split the earnings. We split the work. We split the decisions. No more me making the spreadsheets. No more you making the coffee without asking for help. Equal. Partnership."

"Equal partnership," Maya said. "In a coffee shop."

"In a coffee shop that's about to sell a hundred subscriptions and move 300 pounds of beans and maybe, just maybe, change a neighborhood."

Maya's voice softened. "I'd like that."

"I'd like that too."

He hung up. Maya put the phone down. She stood up. She walked to the window. She stood there and watched the street and thought about Leo driving back to Portland, somewhere on I-5, under the moon and the stars and the particular dark of a highway at night.

She went to bed at 10:30. She slept. She dreamed of Leo's hands on hers, of the roaster at the pop-up, of a chalkboard dragon on a t-shirt, of a future that was no longer a maybe.

After the Seattle phone call, Maya reorganized her life around communication. She started texting Leo with small updates: Sold three subscriptions today, Sam made a new pastry, The espresso machine is making a noise I'm not sure is normal. Leo replied with short affirmations and occasional memes, which Maya found both annoying and comforting. At the shop, Maya started keeping a shared journal with Sam — a cafe notebook where they recorded customer quotes, new blends, and the date the Mystery Bean lottery hit 1,000 tickets. The journal became a time capsule of the turnaround. By the time they were halfway through the notebook, Maya could look back and see exactly how each small decision had compounded into survival. She showed it to Elena when Elena visited from Seattle, and Elena cried. Elena said it reminded her of Leo's childhood journals, the ones where he'd written down every baseball game and every math test with the same meticulous attention to detail. Maya said Leo still did that. Elena said she knew.

Leo's emails became longer. Where his first messages had been short texts and brief apologies, his new emails were two and three pages long, written in paragraphs, with subject lines like "Re: The lease clause" and "Thoughts on subscription logistics" and "Coffee Rings and the future of coffee retail." Maya read them all. She replied selectively. She saved the personal ones in a folder she called "Leo," which she had not renamed since Day One, when she'd created it as a joke. The folder now contained 104 emails. She moved three of them to a subfolder called "Important." She didn't say what they were important about.

Maya also started taking walks in the morning, before the shop opened. She walked the neighborhood with her coffee in a travel mug, watching the street come alive. She noticed things she'd never noticed before: the way the light hit the bakery at 7:12 a.m., the way the Thai restaurant owner swept the sidewalk at 6:58, the way the bookstore stayed dark until 10 because Margaret liked to sleep in. She started a notebook of neighborhood observations, which she called the Alberta Street field guide. By the second week, it had thirty entries. She read from it to Leo when he came over for dinner. He said she should publish it. She said she already was.

The emails from Leo got better. They were no longer just updates. They were observations. He wrote about the light hitting the espresso machine at 6:32. He wrote about the regular who'd started coming in at 8 a.m. and ordering a black coffee and sitting in the same chair for an hour, which Leo said looked like meditation. He wrote about Sam, who'd started painting the walls with a mural of the shop's history, bean by bean. Maya read the emails slowly. She replied with short observations of her own. Her emails were less detailed, but they were hers. One day, Leo replied to one of her short emails with a question: "Can I come over tonight and look at the lease with you?" Maya said yes.

On Thursday, the mayor called personally. Maya didn't recognize the number. She answered cautiously. The voice on the other end said, "This is Mayor Reynolds. I read about your shop." Maya said, "Thank you." The mayor said, "I want to help. But I need to know if this is really about the shop or about the neighborhood." Maya said, "It's about both. The shop is the neighborhood. The neighborhood is the shop." The mayor said, "Good. Because if it's just about one shop, I can't do much. If it's about the neighborhood, I can do a lot." Maya said, "It's about the neighborhood."

By Saturday, Maya had organized a neighborhood meeting at the bookstore. Thirty people showed up. Margaret served cookies. Leo brought coffee. Maya stood at the front and told the story — the order, the lottery, the lease, the petition — and then asked the room what they wanted to fight for. The answer, overwhelmingly, was: the right to stay. The right to have a neighborhood that belonged to them. Maya wrote down every suggestion. At the end of the meeting, they had a list of twelve demands for the city council. Marisol, who had come as a guest, said, "This is how movements start." Maya said, "Then let's start.

By Friday, the cafe notebook had grown so thick that Sam had started a third volume. Maya filled the middle pages with observations about the neighborhood, about customers, about the changing rhythm of the shop. She wrote about the Vietnamese couple who came in every Sunday and ordered two iced coffees and shared a pastry. She wrote about the teenager who came in every day after school and did homework in the corner. She wrote about the man in the Newsboy cap, whose name, she discovered on Tuesday, was Mick. Mick told her he'd been coming to the shop since it opened because the owner reminded him of his daughter. Maya said, "What does your daughter do?" Mick said, "She's a barista in San Francisco." Maya said, "Tell her I said hi." Mick said, "I will."

That evening, Maya and Leo walked to the river. They didn't talk about the shop. They talked about music. Leo said he'd started playing guitar again, something he hadn't done since college. Maya said she used to play piano. They talked about learning to play together. Leo said, "We should start a band." Maya said, "We should not." Leo said, "I'm serious." Maya said, "So am I. The answer is no." Leo laughed. He said, "You're not fun at parties." Maya said, "I'm not fun at coffee shop strategy meetings, either." They walked along the river until their hands found each other. They didn't let go.

By Saturday, the emails from Leo had become letters. Maya could tell the difference because emails ended with "– Leo" and letters ended with nothing. Leo stopped signing his name because he said it felt unnecessary, like signing a letter to your own hand. Maya kept them all in a folder on her laptop, organized by subject line and date. She read them at odd moments: between customers, during afternoon lulls, in the bathroom when she needed to smile. The letters were observations, memories, plans. In one, Leo described waking up at 5:30 a.m. to the sound of rain and thinking about the Oregon Coast. In another, he pasted a printout of a coffee futures chart and wrote two words in the margin: "Interesting data. Call me." In a third, he drew a dragon and labeled it "Sam's spirit animal." Maya forwarded the third letter to Sam with the subject line: "Leo sees you." Sam replied: "I know. Tell him the dragon approves."

Maya also began keeping a second notebook, this one private, where she wrote letters to Leo that she never sent. The first was short: "I miss you. I miss the way you make coffee. I miss the way you say 'math' like it's a swear word. I miss you." She wrote another every day. By the end of the week, she had a stack of unsent letters. She didn't intend to send them. They were practice.
