# Chapter 11 — Business Plans and Bedlam

Maya wrote the business plan at the kitchen table of her apartment, which was above the shop and which she'd been meaning to clean since she'd moved in three years ago. The kitchen table was covered in papers and a laptop and a mug that said "WORLD'S OKAYEST BARISTA" and a pile of clothes that Maya had taken off and then decided not to put away.

She wrote the plan like she was making coffee: methodically, with strong opinions about what belonged in the cup and what didn't.

Section one: the problem. Bean & Leaf was cornering the market. Bean & Leaf had money and branding and a CFO who'd once described coffee as "a consumable product with high margin potential." Maya had once described coffee as "a ritual that makes the morning not terrible."

Section two: the opportunity. The neighborhood wanted what Maya had. They wanted a shop that remembered their names. They wanted coffee that didn't taste like it had been argued about in a boardroom. They wanted a place that was weird and specific and theirs.

Section three: the numbers. Fourteen thousand pounds of Ethiopian Yirgacheffe, two hundred and twelve pounds remaining, three days to sell it. Festival revenue: four thousand dollars. Mystery Bean lottery: fourteen hundred dollars. Loyalty cards: not yet monetized. The Thing: not yet invented.

Section three point five: the thing.

Maya stared at the screen. The thing was not going to be easy. The thing was going to be stupid. The thing was going to be exactly the kind of dumb, beautiful, desperate idea that only happened when you had nothing left to lose.

She'd called it The Coffee Rescue.

The concept: a flash sale on the remaining beans. Not a regular flash sale. A rescue. A last-ditch effort. A community coming together to save something that mattered. She'd price the beans below wholesale. She'd hand-deliver them. She'd pair them with a recipe card that said "YOU SAVED THIS SHOP" and a hug, metaphorically speaking, because actual hugs were a pandemic-era liability.

It was insane. It was beautiful. It might work.

She wrote the full business plan. She wrote it like her life depended on it, because it kind of did. The shop was her grandmother's legacy. The shop was her home. The shop was the reason she'd moved to Portland, had taken the loan, had spent three years serving coffee to strangers and pretending she wasn't lonely.

She finished at 2:00 a.m.

The next morning, she walked downstairs and found Leo already there, sorting the remaining four hundred-pound sacks with the care of someone who was handling something precious.

"Hey," Leo said.

"Hey," Maya said. "I wrote a business plan."

Leo stopped. He looked at her like she'd just performed a magic trick. "A business plan."

"A real one. With numbers. And projections. And a very emotional section about community."

Leo smiled. It was the soft smile, the one that said he was proud of her for reasons that had nothing to do with coffee. "Can I read it."

"Not yet. I need to make a call."

Maya called Mrs. Hsu. She called her at 8:00 a.m., which was a time she'd never called anyone before, because the world was essentially off-limits before 9:00 a.m. and Mrs. Hsu, Maya had learned, operated on her own time zone.

"Mrs. Hsu," Maya said when the phone was answered. "I need to ask you something."

"Ask," Mrs. Hsu said.

"The old pharmacy on 28th. Bean & Leaf bought the lease."

There was a silence. "They did."

"I need to know — if they come for this building. If they come for me. What will you do."

Mrs. Hsu's voice was steady. "I've already answered that."

"You answered that you won't raise my rent. But they'll offer you more. They'll offer you triple. What then."

Mrs. Hsu was quiet for a long moment. The kind of quiet that meant she was choosing her words with the same care Maya chose her beans. "They did offer," she said. "Yesterday. A considerable amount. I told them no."

Maya's throat closed. "You what."

"I told them no. This building has been in my family since 1952. Your grandmother and I played mahjong every Tuesday for fifteen years. She loaned me the money to buy it when I was starting out. I'm not selling to people who want to turn it into a coffee chain."

Tears came to Maya's eyes. She didn't mean for them to. They came anyway. "Thank you," she said.

"Don't thank me yet. Thank me when you've sold those beans."

The call ended. Maya stood in the back room, phone in hand, and cried for exactly thirty seconds. She wiped her face with her sleeve. She walked upstairs.

Leo was waiting. He'd made coffee — her coffee, the Yirgacheffe, the good stuff — and it was steaming on the counter.

"I heard," he said.

"Heard what."

"Mrs. Hsu called me. She wanted to make sure I was helping you. Not hurting you."

Maya's eyes widened. "She called you."

"She's a watchdog, Maya. She's been watching. She told me that if I hurt you, she'd evict me from the shop."

"What did you say."

Leo leaned against the counter, coffee in hand. "I said I was trying not to."

Maya laughed. It was wet, messy, ungraceful. She didn't care. "God," she said. "This is so much."

"I know."

They drank coffee. The shop was quiet. The refrigerator hummed. The espresso machine was asleep. Outside, the street was waking up — a delivery truck, a dog walker, a woman in a yellow raincoat who waved at Maya through the window and Maya waved back because she recognized her from the Mystery Bean lottery.

Maya looked at Leo. He looked tired, but alert. He looked like someone who'd been running and had finally stopped and was trying to figure out what to do next.

"Can I show you something?" she asked.

She led him upstairs to her kitchen table. She opened the laptop. She showed him the business plan.

Leo read it. He read it slowly, pausing at good parts, nodding at numbers, and when he got to the section about The Coffee Rescue, he laughed.

"This is," he said, "the dumbest smart thing I've ever seen."

"Thank you."

"How much do we price the beans."

"Wholesale. Twelve dollars a pound. Below what we paid. We lose money on every bag, but we sell them fast, and the customers feel like they're part of something, and —"

Leo was already reaching for his laptop. "I'm calling every roaster, café, and coffee nerd I know. I'm sending this plan. Today. Right now. We do the rescue. We do it tomorrow."

"Tomorrow," Maya said. "Three days."

"Three days," Leo said. "Let's see what we can do."

They sat at the kitchen table and worked. They wrote emails. They made phone calls. They designed flyers that said "COFFEE RESCUE — HELP US SAVE THE SHOP" and Maya taped them to the window and to every telephone pole within walking distance and to the door of the convenience store across the street, which the owner said was fine as long as Maya bought a pack of gum.

By noon, they'd had seventeen responses. By 2:00 p.m., twenty-four. By 5:00 p.m., thirty-seven. People wanted to help. They wanted to be part of something that mattered. They wanted a story they could tell.

Maya stood at the counter, watching the responses pile up, and for the first time since she'd bought the shop, she didn't feel alone.

She felt like she was part of something bigger. Something that didn't have a name yet. Something that was loud and weird and specific and hers.

"Tomorrow," she said.

"Tomorrow," Leo said.

The bell jingled. Sam walked in. She was holding a phone. "You need to see this," she said.

It was a TikTok. It had forty thousand views. The caption read: "My local coffee shop is about to be destroyed by Bean & Leaf. We're doing a rescue. Who's in."

Maya stared at it. "Who posted this."

"I did," Sam said. "Ten minutes ago."

Maya looked at Sam. Sam looked at Leo. Leo looked at the phone.

"Tomorrow," Leo said again. But this time, he smiled.
